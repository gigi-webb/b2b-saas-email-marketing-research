## Core Philosophy

It costs 5x more to acquire a customer than to keep one, and the probability of selling to an existing customer is 60–70% higher than to a new prospect. Yet most email programs are built for acquisition. Val's framework flips this: retention email is the highest-ROI email work available, and it starts before the product arrives, not after the customer shows signs of leaving. The question that anchors the whole framework: "What would we do to increase MRR if we turned off all acquisition channels?"

## The Framework

### Part 1 — Customer Segmentation (Who You're Emailing)

Val identifies three retention-critical customer types, each requiring a distinct email approach:

**Vampires** — Customers who consume disproportionate support resources and generate low margin. Do *not* build retention flows to keep them; build qualification logic to identify them early.

**Ghosts** — Prospects or early customers who showed genuine interest (signed up, started a trial, made an inquiry) but got distracted before completing the goal. They are not lost — they are busy. Strategy: show up with a consistent cadence (every 2–3 days) in a non-pushy way that keeps the brand top-of-mind until they're ready to re-engage.

**Zombies** — One-time buyers or lapsed customers who purchased once and have not returned. They have a frame of reference for the product and need a specific reason to come back. Strategy: win-back campaigns that "flip the script" — instead of broadcasting "here's what we did," target them with "you asked for this, and we built it."

### Part 2 — The Three Retention Strategies

**Strategy 1 — Go beyond traditional support**  
When a customer submits feedback ("I wish you had feature X"), most companies send a generic acknowledgment. The retention play: tag the feedback, build a segment of everyone who requested that feature, and send a personal email when it ships. Example: Customer.io did this explicitly — "When we last spoke, you were interested but needed this feature. We built it." The copy signals that the company remembered the individual.

**Strategy 2 — Be human**  
Text-based emails from a named person consistently outperform designed HTML templates for relationship-building. Invite customers to reply. Ask open-ended questions. When they reply, respond personally and invite them to a brief interview call (incentivise with a third-party gift card, not a brand discount, to avoid transactional framing). The data from customer interviews produces exact customer language that can be recycled into future email copy.

**Strategy 3 — Build the habit loop (Jobs to Be Done)**  
Identify: What problem were customers solving when they found your product? What triggers them to act? What does an early win look like, and what keeps them coming back? Map these to post-purchase email flows. Reference: James Clear's *Atomic Habits* habit loop — cue → craving → response → reward — applied to the subscription lifecycle.

### Part 3 — The Dinner Party Strategy (Email Sequencing Model)

Don't lead with the main course. A dinner party flows: welcome → drinks and appetisers → side dishes → main course → dessert → invitation to return. Applied to a post-purchase or welcome flow:

1. **Welcome / Check in** — Acknowledge they just made a decision; make them feel like they arrived somewhere good
2. **Educate** — Pre-delivery: tell them what to expect, how to prepare, what others have found. Post-delivery: onboarding and early-win guidance
3. **Build relationship** — Human note, question, empathy moment (optional opt-out for campaigns like Black Friday)
4. **Product / conversion ask** — Now that trust is established, introduce upgrade paths, subscription options, or referral programmes
5. **Win-back** — After a defined lapse period, send a short, direct re-engagement email; text-based often outperforms designed

### Part 4 — Post-Purchase Email Architecture (Three Flows)

| Flow | Trigger | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-delivery | Order confirmed, product not yet arrived | Education, expectation-setting, reduce purchase regret |
| Post-delivery | Product arrives | Onboarding, review request (2nd purchase, not 1st), care instructions |
| Long-term use | 30/60/90 days post-purchase | Referral programme, subscription offer, loyalty milestone |

**Rule:** Never ask for a review on the first purchase welcome email. Never introduce a referral programme before the customer has used the product enough to have a genuine opinion.

### Part 5 — Deliverability as Retention Signal

Email engagement is a proxy for inbox placement. Sends that generate replies, clicks, and forward behaviour signal to inbox providers that messages are wanted — which protects deliverability for subscribers who don't engage every time. This means retention email tactics (reply invitations, question-asking, empathy campaigns) also protect the overall program's technical health.

## Key Quote from Transcript

> "All human beings want is to feel seen and heard. That's all anybody wants… if you as a brand can provide a way for your customers, your subscribers, to feel seen and heard, that creates a connection that then makes it really hard to go buy [from a competitor]."

And on the "flip the script" tactic:

> "Instead of saying 'we put bigger pockets in our shorts'… you can say to your zombie customers who requested bigger pockets in the shorts: 'you asked for this and we made it.' Which one is more compelling to you? The one that says you specifically asked for this."

## Application for B2B SaaS

1. **Segment your current list** into Ghosts (trial started, no conversion), Zombies (churned or lapsed paid), and Active. Build one dedicated flow per segment before running any broadcast campaigns.

2. **Audit your post-purchase/post-signup flow** against the Dinner Party model. If your welcome email leads with product features, you're serving the main course before drinks — restructure to build relationship before pitching.

3. **Tag all support and feedback emails** by product feature requested. When that feature ships, send a personal email to everyone who requested it explicitly naming their request: "You told us X. We built it." This is the highest-trust product announcement email available.

4. **Add a reply prompt to your next send.** Text-based email from a named person, one open question, sent from a monitored inbox. Track the replies. The exact words customers use in replies are your next campaign copy.

5. **Build a Black Friday / peak period opt-out campaign** — offering a temporary opt-out while previewing what's coming. Counter-intuitively, these emails consistently generate revenue from subscribers who click through out of curiosity despite intending to opt out.
