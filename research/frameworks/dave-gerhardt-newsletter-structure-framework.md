# Dave Gerhardt — B2B Newsletter Operating Framework

**Source:** research/youtube-transcripts/dave-gerhardt/how-to-crush-email-marketing-b2b-2026.md  
**Expert:** Dave Gerhardt, Founder of Exit 5 (formerly CMO at Drift, co-founder of Privy)  
**Note:** This framework synthesises insights from four Exit 5 live session panelists — Dave Gerhardt, Jana (Knack), Kmy (fractional B2B email marketer), and Ben (Quo/OpenPhone). It represents the operating model that emerged independently across their combined experience.

---

## Core Philosophy

A B2B newsletter is not a marketing channel — it is a trust product. The distinction matters because channels optimise for reach and conversion events, while trust products optimise for relationship depth and long-term buying intent. Most B2B companies treat their newsletter as a distribution mechanism for blog posts and product announcements. The companies seeing the strongest results treat it as a primary editorial product: something a subscriber would miss if it stopped arriving.

The operating principle: "Assume nobody wants your marketing. Given that, what would make your email worth reading anyway?"

## The Framework

### Module 1 — Positioning Decision (Do This Before Everything Else)

Before choosing cadence, template, or topics, answer one question in a single sentence:

**"Our newsletter makes [specific reader type] better at [specific job to be done]."**

Examples that work:
- "The Exit 5 newsletter makes B2B marketers better at running campaigns that actually get results, by sharing real examples from practitioners — not theory."
- A "confidence factory" (Katelyn Bourgoin's framing) — every issue should make the reader more confident about one thing they need to do.

If you cannot write this sentence clearly, editorial decisions will drift toward what's easy for the brand to produce rather than what's valuable to the reader.

### Module 2 — Operating Model (Four Pillars)

**Pillar 1 — The Quarterback**  
One named person owns the newsletter: editorial calendar, theme selection, writer coordination, cadence enforcement. This is the single point of accountability. Not a committee. Not rotating ownership. The quarterback does not have to write every issue — but they make every editorial decision and are responsible for ensuring the issue ships on time.

**Pillar 2 — Rotating Bylines**  
Different team members write different issues under their own name. Benefits: distributes the production burden; surfaces authentic individual voices; builds the reader's relationship with multiple humans at the organisation, not just the brand. The demand gen lead, the content writer, the designer, the CEO — each writes from their actual expertise.

**Pillar 3 — Fixed Template, Variable Content**  
Never redesign the newsletter. The template is invisible infrastructure — readers don't read it, they read through it. Redesigning wastes production time and breaks reader habituation. The only thing that changes between issues is the content.

A proven B2B newsletter template (adapted from Kmy's framework):
1. Campaign highlight or timely hook (2–3 sentences, above fold)
2. Personal note from the byline author — conversational, first person, something the reader actually cares about (not "the company is excited to announce")
3. Curated third-party content with original commentary ("This is interesting because X, and here's what it means for you")
4. Soft conversion element — event, resource, or product mention with a clear value framing
5. Author photo, name, title, and a reply invitation

**Pillar 4 — Work 2–3 Weeks Ahead**  
Never write the newsletter in the week it ships. The panic of week-of production degrades quality and collapses the editor review cycle. The quarterback maintains a 2–3 week buffer. This also provides strategic flexibility: a major industry event, a trending topic, or a practitioner guest can be inserted without displacing the production schedule.

### Module 3 — Cadence as Brand Signal

Treat the newsletter like an old-school TV show: same day, same time, every issue. The reader may not open every issue — but the brand name appearing in their inbox on the same day each week is a touchpoint even when unopened. Over time, this cadence creates a Pavlovian association: "Tuesday at 8am" becomes synonymous with the brand.

The cadence decision:
- **Weekly:** Requires a strong editorial team. Best for communities with daily active members (Exit 5 model)
- **Bi-weekly:** Allows deeper, longer-form content with higher production value (Emily Kramer / MKT1 model)
- **Monthly:** Appropriate for small teams; lower touchpoint frequency reduces trust-building speed

Do not change the cadence. Reader habituation is one of the core assets of a newsletter program and it takes months to build.

### Module 4 — Content Strategy

**The 70/30 rule (adapted from Jana's framework):**
- 70% genuinely useful content — not promotional, not product-centric. This includes curating excellent content from other creators, competitors, and the broader industry. Featuring content from sources outside the company builds trust faster than featuring only your own content.
- 30% soft promotion — your events, your products, your content, framed as useful resources rather than CTAs

**The "give before you take" principle (Kmy's framing):**  
Earn the right to ask. A newsletter that consistently delivers value earns the occasional conversion email. A newsletter that leads with brand promotion on every issue trains readers to stop opening.

**Original POV requirement:**  
Every piece of curated content needs the author's interpretation: "This is relevant because X, and here's what I think it means for you." A newsletter that only curates without commentary is a content aggregator, not a trust product.

### Module 5 — Metrics (What to Actually Measure)

**Primary metric: Reply rate**  
Count replies per issue. Route all replies to a monitored inbox (not no-reply@). Even 1–5 replies on a list of a few hundred is a strong signal — replying to a brand email is a significant behavioural commitment that readers rarely make unless the content genuinely landed. Reply volume by topic also functions as a qualitative content compass.

**Secondary metric: Unsubscribe rate by issue**  
Spikes indicate a topic, tone, or frequency misstep. Track unsubscribes by issue, not just by month.

**Do not use as the primary metric:** Open rate (inflated by Apple MPP), click-through rate (captures intent but misses the majority of readers who absorb content without clicking).

**The "silent engager" phenomenon:** In Kmy's enterprise client case, the company tracked email opens in the 10 weeks before subscribers converted to buyers — and found that silent readers who had never clicked a CTA were among the most likely to purchase. Email engagement precedes buying behaviour; it just doesn't show up in standard dashboards.

### Module 6 — Personalization Layer (Advanced)

Once the operating model is stable, add one personalisation element per quarter:

**Year-in-review / Spotify Wrapped email (from Ben's Quo case):**  
Pull product usage data for each customer, generate personalised metrics (calls made, features used, busiest day), and deliver as a personal "data story" email. This required 110 lines of SQL, a 4-month build, and an agency partner — but generated the highest engagement of any campaign that year and viral social sharing.

**Newsjacking sequences (Tyler's system):**  
For B2B products with long sales cycles (12–18 months), use AI to monitor industry news → generate email briefs within hours of relevant events → send timely, reactive emails that demonstrate the company is paying attention to the reader's world. Example: 67.3% open rate on a pharmacy client's FDA regulatory update email sent within one hour of the news breaking.

## Application for B2B SaaS

**Week 1: Foundation**
- Write your single positioning sentence
- Appoint a quarterback (this is the most important decision — a newsletter without an owner fails within 3 months)
- Choose cadence and lock in the publish day/time
- Set up a reply monitoring inbox (Help Scout, Front, or any shared inbox tool)

**Week 2–4: First 3 issues**
- Use a fixed template from day one — do not iterate on the template
- Write 2–3 issues before sending the first one (you need the buffer)
- Source your first round of non-company-content to curate (3–5 high-quality pieces per issue)

**Month 2–3: Measurement**
- Track replies per issue manually
- Track unsubscribes per issue
- Survey 5–10 subscribers via direct email: "What would you miss most if this newsletter stopped?"

**Month 4+: Growth**
- Introduce cross-recommendations with complementary B2B newsletters
- Experiment with one guest byline per quarter
- Evaluate platform-specific growth levers (Substack recommendations, Beehiiv's network, LinkedIn newsletter cross-promotion)
