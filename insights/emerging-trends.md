# Emerging Trends in B2B SaaS Email Marketing

**Extracted from:** 14 YouTube transcripts across 10 experts  
**Research period:** 2024–2026  
**Last updated:** June 21, 2026

---

## Overview

These five trends were identified by cross-referencing signals that appeared independently across multiple experts and transcripts — not single mentions. Each trend includes supporting evidence from the source transcripts and a practitioner implication.

---

## Trend 1: AI as Email Co-Pilot — From Copywriter to System Builder

**Signal strength: Very High** (5 of 10 experts, 6+ transcripts)

### What's happening

AI is moving from optional writing assistant to the operational backbone of email workflows. The frontier has shifted from "use AI to write a draft" to "build AI systems that monitor, brief, write, design, and deliver emails faster than any human team."

The most concrete example came from an Exit 5 live session: a B2B lifecycle marketer built a five-step newsjacking system using Claude + Google Alerts that collapses trend monitoring, email brief creation, copywriting, and design QA into under one hour. A pharmaceutical client email sent within an hour of an FDA regulatory change achieved a **67.3% open rate and 4% reply rate** — driven entirely by timeliness.

Emily Kramer (MKT1, 75K+ subscribers) uses Claude Code for subject line generation, copy editing, cross-linking old newsletters, and diagram creation via the Figma MCP server. She reports spending *more* time on content quality as AI handles the mechanical production work: "It gives me more time to spend on the other stuff."

Kath Pay (Holistic Email Marketing, 27 years experience) runs a privately trained Claude instance loaded with her proprietary frameworks and 27 years of email psychology content. She uses it to generate persuasion-principle-driven copy (Cialdini's six principles, cognitive biases, bimodal buyer personas) and writes AB test hypotheses via AI. Her framing: "We are the director of the customer's journey. AI is our eager intern."

**New capability unlocked:** A single email marketer running Claude + MCP + Figma can now operate like a small team — briefing, writing, designing, and QA-ing an on-brand email in under 60 minutes.

### Practitioner implication

Stop using AI only at the sentence level. Build repeatable *systems* (monitoring → brief → draft → design → QA) that AI runs end-to-end. The marketers winning now are treating Claude as a workflow engine, not a writing tool.

**Sources:** Dave Gerhardt Exit 5 live transcript (Tyler's newsjacking system); Emily Kramer MKT1 newsletter interview; Kath Pay holistic email + Gen AI transcripts

## Trend 2: Editorial Newsletters as B2B Trust Infrastructure

**Signal strength: Very High** (Emily Kramer, Dave Gerhardt, Katelyn Bourgoin + multiple panelists)

### What's happening

The framing has flipped: newsletters are no longer a distribution tactic for blog posts — they are the primary trust-building channel in B2B, sitting above social and below direct sales conversations in the relationship stack.

Two panelists at a Dave Gerhardt live session (one from Knack, one a fractional email marketer) independently made the same argument: "B2B newsletters are the most slept-on channel to build trust with your audience." Their evidence: introducing a bi-weekly newsletter to a 10,000-contact dormant B2B list produced consistent 40–50% open rates and influenced nearly $500K in revenue over two years. For a larger enterprise brand (100K+ contacts), restructuring from campaign blasts to a nurture newsletter reduced unsubscribes by 20% and increased click-through by 40% in under three months.

The operating model that makes this work: fixed template (only content varies), rotating writers across the marketing team, a single quarterback who owns cadence and theme, working 2–3 weeks ahead. The cadence is treated like a scheduled TV show — same day, same time, every week — so the brand name in the inbox becomes a touchpoint even when the reader doesn't open.

Emily Kramer's MKT1 newsletter (75K+ subscribers) is built on long-form essays with original diagrams. Her Substack recommendations feature drives 30–40% of subscriber growth, demonstrating the network effect when the newsletter itself is the product rather than a promotional vehicle.

**New framing:** The newsletter is not a marketing channel — it's the product that earns permission to sell later.

### Practitioner implication

Treat the newsletter as a media operation with an editorial calendar, a named author, and reader-service goals. Measure success by reply rates and DMs, not open rates. Feature other people's best content (competitors included) — trust compounds faster when you curate the industry, not just your own output.

**Sources:** Emily Kramer interview transcript; Dave Gerhardt Exit 5 live (Jana and Kmy presentations); Katelyn Bourgoin newsletter growth transcript

## Trend 3: Behavioral Lifecycle Segmentation Replacing Batch-and-Blast

**Signal strength: High** (Val Geisler, Kath Pay, Brennan Dunn)

### What's happening

The industry is moving from list-size thinking (how many emails can I send?) to lifecycle-stage targeting (what does this person need at this moment?). Three experts with very different backgrounds converged on the same architecture independently.

Val Geisler introduced the Vampire/Ghost/Zombie framework: Vampires are costly customers who drain resources; Ghosts are prospects who showed interest but got distracted (re-engage with cadenced emails); Zombies are one-time buyers who lapsed (win-back campaigns that flip the script from "here's what we did" to "you asked for this and we built it"). Her core insight: selling to an existing customer is 60–70% more likely to succeed than to a new prospect. Email automation should be built around these segments before any campaign sends.

Kath Pay frames the same idea as lifecycle marketing strategy: the overarching email strategy should be documented across acquisition, conversion, retention, win-back, and loyalty stages — not just a quarterly tactic calendar. She found a client with persistent deliverability problems: the root cause was emailing users from free trials taken 5+ years ago who had never engaged. The fix was lifecycle hygiene, not creative optimization.

Brennan Dunn (RightMessage) built an entire product category around behavioral personalization — serving different email content based on user actions within the product, not demographic tags. His insight: because each email is an individual entity, you can personalize at a one-to-one level that no other channel allows.

### Practitioner implication

Audit your segments before your next campaign. Map your current subscriber base to lifecycle stages (trial, active, at-risk, churned, never-engaged). Build automations for each stage before expanding broadcast frequency. The highest ROI email work is lifecycle automation, not campaign volume.

**Sources:** Val Geisler customer retention transcript; Kath Pay holistic email strategy transcript; Brennan Dunn email personalization transcript

## Trend 4: Reply Rate as the New North Star Metric

**Signal strength: High** (Dave Gerhardt, multiple practitioners)

### What's happening

Open rates are broken (Apple MPP inflated them artificially), click-through rates measure the wrong thing (a conversion intent, not relationship strength), and neither metric captures the B2B buyer who reads every email but never clicks until they're ready to buy.

At the Dave Gerhardt Exit 5 session, two panelists independently stopped measuring open/click metrics for their newsletters and started counting replies and DMs instead. One tracked how many emails buyers had opened in the 10 weeks *before* they converted — finding that silent readers who never clicked CTAs were among the most likely to purchase. The takeaway: email engagement is often invisible in standard dashboards but present in the buyer's decision process.

Dave Gerhardt gets "dozens of replies every week" from his Exit 5 newsletter. He uses reply volume as a directional signal to identify which topics resonate, then shares that signal with his content team. Even 1–2 replies on a list of a few hundred people signals meaningful engagement — replying to a brand newsletter requires overcoming strong social conditioning against it.

The operational implication is routing all newsletter replies to a monitored inbox (e.g., via Help Scout), tagging by topic and issue number, and using that data to steer editorial decisions — a feedback loop most teams never build.

### Practitioner implication

Add a reply CTA to every newsletter: "Reply and tell me X." Route all replies to a monitored inbox. Track reply counts by issue. A 0.5% reply rate on a list of 1,000 (5 replies) is a strong signal. Build a reply-rate dashboard alongside opens and clicks.

**Sources:** Dave Gerhardt Exit 5 live session (Jana, Kmy, Dave's own comments); Kmy's B2B newsletter restructuring case

## Trend 5: Product Data as Email Personalization Fuel

**Signal strength: Medium-High** (Dave Gerhardt live session, Val Geisler, Brennan Dunn)

### What's happening

The most memorable email campaigns of 2025–2026 are not written from scratch — they are generated *from product usage data*. The Spotify Wrapped model has arrived in B2B SaaS email.

A lifecycle marketer at a business phone platform shared the most concrete example: a "Year in Review" email campaign that sent each customer a fully personalized animated GIF and a set of real data visualizations (calls made, messages sent, busiest call time) pulled via Snowflake queries, bucketed into four personas (Dial Dynamo, Message Maestro, AI Innovator, Platform Innovator), and rendered via a Chart.js API pipeline into Customer.io. Users received shareable landing pages. The campaign required 110 lines of SQL and a four-month build. It generated viral sharing and "fantastic responses" with very low unsubscribes.

Val Geisler pointed to the same principle at the copy level: rather than writing "we launched this feature," write "you asked for this feature and we built it." The email becomes evidence that the company was listening — which converts zombie customers (lapsed one-time buyers) better than any promotional offer.

Brennan Dunn built RightMessage specifically to let email platforms access behavioral signals (what pages a user visited, what features they triggered, what plan tier they're on) and personalize both email content and on-site messaging dynamically.

**New capability:** AI now makes the data pipeline cheaper — one or two engineers can build the Wrapped-style campaign that previously required a whole team and four months. The bottleneck has shifted from technical to strategic: do you have the data, the creative concept, and the recipient segments mapped?

### Practitioner implication

Audit what product data you currently have access to (logins, feature usage, session counts, milestones). Find one personalization angle (busiest day, most-used feature, progress toward a goal) that would make your customer feel *seen*. Build a single data-driven email this quarter. Even a plain-text "You've done X this month — here's what that means" outperforms a generic broadcast.

**Sources:** Dave Gerhardt Exit 5 live (Ben's Year in Review campaign); Val Geisler retention transcript ("flip the script" and zombie customers); Brennan Dunn RightMessage transcripts

---

## Cross-Trend Pattern

All five trends share a common root: **the best email marketers are moving from volume-based thinking to signal-based thinking**. Instead of asking "how many emails can I send?", they are asking "what does this person need to hear right now, and how do I know it worked?" AI, lifecycle segmentation, product data, editorial newsletters, and reply-rate tracking are all expressions of the same underlying shift.
